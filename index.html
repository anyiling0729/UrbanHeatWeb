Research Foundation

Research Background

Understanding the intersection of urban heat islands and social vulnerability

Environmental Justice Crisis

Urban heat exposure has emerged as a critical environmental justice concern in the context of accelerating climate change. Vulnerable populations—such as low-income, racialized, and socially disadvantaged groups—often face disproportionate exposure to extreme urban temperatures due to systemic inequalities in land use, housing, and infrastructure planning.

Critical Issue

Systemic Thermal Inequality

These groups are more likely to live in neighborhoods with fewer trees, higher building density, and limited access to cooling infrastructure, which exacerbates their vulnerability during heat events. Such environmental disadvantages are frequently coupled with social and economic stressors.

High Impact

Research Gaps

While some studies have explored the correlates of UHI intensity, few have systematically quantified the role of specific environmental features in shaping thermal inequities across space and time. There is limited understanding of how urban features interact with social vulnerability.

Research Need

Machine Learning Opportunities

Recent advances in interpretable machine learning offer new opportunities to unpack complex, non-linear interactions among environmental predictors and social variables. ML models can capture non-linear relationships and higher-order interactions that are difficult to detect using traditional methods.

Innovation

Research Questions

Research Approach

Research Methodology

A comprehensive framework combining machine learning and spatial analysis

1
Multi-Scenario Modeling Framework

Build UHI prediction models for four season-time scenarios: Summer Daytime (SD), Summer Nighttime (SN), Winter Daytime (WD), Winter Nighttime (WN) using satellite-derived land surface temperature data.

2
Ensemble Machine Learning

Use AutoGluon framework to build ensemble regression models, including CatBoost, neural networks (NN Torch), and Extremely Randomized Trees (XT) with automated bagging and multi-layer stacking strategies.

3
Intervention Strategy Simulation

Simulate intervention scenarios by scaling environmental features from 0.0x to 2.0x, evaluating cooling effects and equity impacts across socio-demographic groups.

4 Time Scenarios
Satellite LST Data Source
Area Weighted Aggregation Method

UHI values were computed for four representative scenarios in 2018 using satellite-derived land surface temperature (LST) datasets, aggregated to the DA level using area-weighted averaging.

Environmental Features (7)

  • Building Coverage Ratio (BCR): Ratio of total building footprint area to total DA area
  • Mean Building Height: Area-weighted average height across all buildings within each DA
  • Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI): Computed from Sentinel-2 bands and aggregated using area-weighted averaging
  • Water Body Distance: Proximity to lakes or ponds, calculated using overlay analysis
  • Watercourse Distance: Proximity to rivers, providing linear water system cooling effects
  • POI Density: Point of interest density, proxy for human activity and infrastructure concentration
  • Road Density: Road network density, often correlating with impervious surface coverage

Socio-demographic Features (4)

  • Unemployment Rate: Percentage of labor force aged 15+ who are unemployed
  • Low-Income Population Ratio: Proportion of individuals below the low-income threshold as defined by Canadian census
  • No High School Diploma Ratio: Proportion of individuals aged 25+ without a secondary school diploma
  • Visible Minority Ratio: Proportion of individuals identified as visible minority members (Statistics Canada definition)

The Summer Day model performs best with a test R² of 0.7873, indicating strong generalization ability. The Summer Night model shows lower performance (R² = 0.5348), likely reflecting increased complexity of nighttime heat dynamics.

Feature Importance Analysis
Key Environmental Drivers:
  • NDVI (Vegetation Index): Most important predictor across all scenarios, particularly effective in Summer Daytime
  • BCR (Building Coverage Ratio): Strong predictor of urban density, with importance varying by season
  • Watercourse Distance: Ranks in top three in most models, especially important at night
  • Water Body Distance: Provides complementary cooling effects, particularly in summer
  • Building Height: Importance varies by scenario, more influential at night
  • POI Density: Stronger correlation at night, reflecting anthropogenic heat emissions
  • Road Density: Related to impervious surface coverage and transportation heat

Vegetation coverage and proximity to water bodies are the most effective strategies for mitigating urban heat island effects, with seasonal and diurnal variations in their effectiveness.

Key Insights

Systemic Thermal Exposure Inequality

Visible minority and no-education-certificate groups show positive correlations in all scenarios, indicating systemic thermal vulnerability that persists across seasons and times.

Temporal Vulnerability Dependencies

Low-income groups show negative correlations during summer days but positive correlations during nights and winter, indicating complex temporal dependencies in thermal vulnerability.

Equity of Nature-Based Interventions

Nature-based strategies (greening and water proximity) not only provide the strongest cooling effects but also ensure the fairest distribution of benefits across social groups.

Theoretical contributions, practical significance, and future research directions

Key Theoretical Contributions
  • Extends climate justice discourse by integrating urban morphology, social vulnerability, and data-driven modeling approaches
  • Reveals that thermal inequality is not only socio-economic but also spatially embedded in the built environment configuration
  • Provides new perspectives for studying the intersection of spatial forms and social inequality
  • Enriches existing environmental justice theory with spatial analysis perspectives and machine learning insights
Practical Significance
  • Provides actionable equity-oriented mitigation strategies for urban planning and climate adaptation
  • Supports data-driven, equity-oriented decision making in urban development
  • Framework is transferable to other cities, supporting global urban climate adaptation efforts
  • Consistent with Sustainable Development Goals (SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and SDG 13: Climate Action)
Core Findings Summary
Nature-Based Intervention Strategies

Nature-based strategies (especially urban greening and water proximity) not only provide the strongest cooling effects (up to 2.95°C) but also ensure the fairest distribution of benefits across social groups.

Equity Trade-offs

Some built environment adjustments, while potentially effective for cooling, may exacerbate existing inequalities and require equity-oriented policy safeguards and careful calibration.

Temporal Dependencies

Thermal vulnerability is not static but varies with seasons and time of day, requiring dynamic adaptation strategies that account for spatiotemporal complexity.

Future Research Directions
Longitudinal Research Design

Incorporate decade-long climate trends and extreme heat events to understand temporal dynamics

Causal Inference

Use structural equation modeling or natural experiments to strengthen causal understanding

High-Resolution Data

Integrate IoT sensors or crowdsourced platform data for finer spatial resolution

Cross-City Comparison

Extend framework to other cities and climate conditions for broader applicability

Research Limitations
  • Geographic Scope: Limited to one city (Vancouver) and one year (2018)
  • Model Performance: Overfitting phenomena exist in certain periods (Summer Night, Winter Day)
  • Causality: Research is essentially correlational, requiring further causal mechanism research
  • Policy Variables: Potential key variables such as policies, history, and behavior are not directly modeled
  • Endogeneity: Endogenous relationships between features (e.g., vegetation and income)
  • Interpretability: AutoML interpretability remains limited despite advances
